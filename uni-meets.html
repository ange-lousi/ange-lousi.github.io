The Problem

University students struggle to make friendships.

Humans are inherently social creatures. We crave a form of belonging and a sense of connectedness that has been hard-wired into our behavior for survival since the beginning of human evolution. So as a university student, the rigor of college and its ever-changing schedules make it difficult to connect with other students, especially those who want to communicate with others but struggle to make that first step due to personal barriers. As a senior, I noticed how many of my peers, especially the juniors, found it difficult to find this sense of belonging and connectedness, sparking the question-why was there such disconnect in our lives? And how might we help university students make friends faster?

The Solution

UniMeets: an events-based networking app that connects students through shared interests.

The goal of my project was to provide a headstart for students who have a hard time forming friendships, whether it be due to their fast-changing schedules or their struggle with social interaction; I wanted to create a safe platform for students who wish to make genuine friends. So I tried to rethink how students could engage in more fun, meaningful, and productive ways to build those friendships and form sincere connections.

UniMeets is an events-based networking app that allows users to create profiles and add interests that generate events based on the user's interests. Users need to mark an event they want to attend where they will be grouped with other students who will also be in attendance. From there, they will automatically be added to a group chat where they can introduce themselves and get to know one another, to eventually attend the event and meet peers from their school.

Competitive analysis

The competition had NO audience specific to university students.

I began doing some research to see what's currently out there. I noted down the four most popular platforms, identified vital features I envisioned UniMeets to have, and reassessed existing platforms based on those features. To my surprise, various friend apps also included an event-based element. However, no specific event app caters explicitly to university students. Although there are also social media platforms such as Instagram and Facebook, those sites are more for maintaining friendships than creating new ones.

Design

Setbacks + a new direction for new friendships.

At first, I spent the first 2 - 3 weeks brainstorming, wireframing, and designing a social networking app solely catered towards uni students. But I kept hesitating on the idea because I thought, if I did that, how would it be any different from any other social networking apps out there? Then I figured, maybe Augmented Reality? But then, not everyone will have access to the equipment, which would become more of a hassle than a benefit. Plus, the same effects that you could have using AR could be used through a simple smartphone app. So I decided to beef up the social networking approach instead by catering to events and using a topic that will make people come together based on what they are interested in, which would be a great icebreaker when making friends. Hence why I stuck with an app for my final solution.

Conclusion

Reflections + what I'd do differently next time.

This was my first-ever solo UX project ðŸ¥³. More than the actual output, I'm grateful to have been through an entire UX process, so I can see what it's actually like. Here are a few things I've learned:

  • Iterate as much as you can. I explored many different options in the beginning stages to find the right solution for my users. I ended up "restarting" my project three times via brainstorming and wireframing to ensure that every aspect of the app was made with intention.
  • Be decisive. After I decided on the direction of this app and began the research process, it became very tempting to get sidetracked by adding more features to the design. Sometimes a new idea would come to mind, and I would be tempted to revise the entire plan because I thought of a completely different approach. Hence, in the future, I believe concentrating on an idea or theory that solves the pain points before branching off towards other features, reducing the completion time of the case study.
  • The only real mistake is the one from which we learn nothing. From catching errors in my UI to uncovering more foundational UX problems in my app, I am thankful for the Human-Computer Interaction course I did at uni and the various online resources which I could fall back on when I needed guidance. Though there is much more room for improvement, I have constructed the app as best I could and did not let my thoughts and opinions stop me from questioning if my own decisions were truly best for the user.
